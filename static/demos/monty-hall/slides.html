The Monty Hall Problem

Why you should always switch doors

A question from my master's interview

The professor asked me a probability puzzle.

Three doors. One car. Two goats.

You pick a door. The host reveals a goat behind another.

Do you switch or stay?

My answer: "It doesn't matter. 50/50."

My reasoning: the host opening a door is an independent event. It shouldn't affect whether my original choice was correct.

Sounds reasonable. But it's wrong.

The host knows where the car is.
He will never open the door with the car.

Not independent

If you picked a goat door (2/3 of the time), the host has no choice. He must open the only other goat door.

The remaining door must have the car.

His action is constrained by yours.
He's leaking information.

The math

1/3

2/3

Stay = win when your first pick was right
Switch = win when your first pick was wrong

Still not convinced? Try 100 doors.

You pick one. The host opens 98 doors, all goats.
Would you switch to the one door he deliberately left closed?

A famously controversial answer

In 1990, Marilyn vos Savant published the answer in her column.

She received ~10,000 letters telling her she was wrong — many from mathematicians.

She wasn't.

"When your intuition says 'obviously,' that's exactly when you should slow down and check."

The Monty Hall problem taught me that being confident and being correct are two very different things.
